**Problem 3**: The key is to observe that $\left(|\mathbf{r}(t)|^2\right)'=2\mathbf{r}(t)\cdot \mathbf{r}'(t)$.

**Problem 5**: Many students misunderstood the question. Since the engine is shut off at time $t=0$, My Skywalker is going to have a linear motion. His initial position is determined by $\mathbf{r}(0)$ and his velocity is just $\mathbf{r}'(0)$. Thus to determine whether he can make to the station is to check if the vector between his initial position and the station is parallel to his velocity.

**Problem 6**: Some students found the distance from the fly&#8217;s initial point to the point where it hits the sphere. But here what we really want to find is the arc length between those two points along the curve.

**Problem 10**: The first thing to observe is that the curve is actually a line even it does not look like one. Since $x(t)=t^3, y(t)=2t^3, z(t)=-t^3$, we always have $2x=y=-2z$ which is exactly the symmetric equation for a line. Once notice this, the rest is to parametrize the line (forget about the old parametric equation of $t$) such that the speed is 2. Note this line goes through the origin and its direction is $(1,2,-1)$. In other words, the &#8216;velocity&#8217; now is $(1,2,-1)$ and we want to find a new &#8216;velocity&#8217; such that its magnitude, i.e., speed, is 2. So the new &#8216;velocity&#8217; is $\frac{2}{\sqrt{6}}(1,2,-1)$. Thus the new parametric equation is $r(s)=\frac{2s}{\sqrt{6}}(1,2,-1)$.
